Poincaré duality is one of the most satisfying facts about manifolds: on a compact oriented
manifold, topology in dimension $i$ is paired with topology in dimension $n-i$. But singular
spaces are less polite. A singular point can behave like a shortcut through which chains pass too
freely, and ordinary homology has no memory of how a chain used the singular set.

Intersection homology, introduced by Goresky and MacPherson, fixes this by changing the chain
complex rather than changing the space. The slogan is:

> cycles may approach singularities only in controlled dimension.

The point of this post is to explain what that means, why it restores duality, and how the
suspension of a torus shows the failure and the repair in the smallest interesting example.

## Duality for Smooth Manifolds

Let $M$ be a compact, connected, oriented $n$-manifold. Poincaré duality says that cap product
with the fundamental class gives an isomorphism

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`H_i(M; \QQ) \xrightarrow{\ \cap [M]\ } H_{n-i}(M; \QQ).`} />

Equivalently, over a field, there is a perfect pairing

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`H_i(M; \QQ) \times H_{n-i}(M; \QQ) \longrightarrow \QQ.`} />

Geometrically, put an $i$-cycle and an $(n-i)$-cycle in general position. Their intersection is a
finite signed set of points, and counting those points gives a number depending only on the two
homology classes.

That proof leans on a hidden local fact: every point of a manifold has a Euclidean neighborhood.
When cycles are perturbed into general position, they stay inside a world where transversality and
orientation behave exactly as expected. Singular spaces break that local input.

## The torus suspension as a warning

Consider

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`X = \Sigma T^2.`} />

Topologically, this is obtained from $T^2 \times [-1,1]$ by collapsing each end torus to a point.
Away from the two cone points, $X$ looks like a $3$-manifold. At either cone point, however, the
link is $T^2$, not $S^2$, so $X$ is not a manifold.

Ordinary reduced homology of a suspension satisfies

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\widetilde H_j(\Sigma Y; \QQ) \cong \widetilde H_{j-1}(Y; \QQ).`} />

Since $H_1(T^2; \QQ) \cong \QQ^2$ and $H_2(T^2; \QQ) \cong \QQ$, the ordinary homology of
$X = \Sigma T^2$ is:

| $i$ | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $H_i(X; \QQ)$ | $\QQ$ | $0$ | $\QQ^2$ | $\QQ$ |

If $X$ behaved like a closed oriented $3$-manifold, Poincaré duality would force $b_1=b_2$.
Instead $b_1=0$ and $b_2=2$.

What happened? A loop on the equatorial torus can be filled by coning it to a singular point.
From the smooth part of the space, this is a very bad filling: the whole loop has been crushed
through one singular point.

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/blog/intersection-homology/figure-2.png" alt="A loop on the torus cross-section filled by coning it to a singular point." />
  <figcaption>Ordinary homology allows a loop on the torus cross-section to be filled by coning it to a singular point.</figcaption>
</figure>

This is the basic pathology. Ordinary homology only asks whether a chain exists. It does not ask
whether the chain used the singularity in a controlled way.

There is also an algebraic view. Write $X$ as the union of two cones:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`X = C_+ \cup_{T^2} C_- .`} />

Each cone is contractible, so the Mayer-Vietoris sequence forces

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`H_2(X; \QQ) \cong H_1(T^2; \QQ) \cong \QQ^2, \qquad H_1(X; \QQ)=0.`} />

The same story appears in exact-sequence language: the cone pieces kill the one-cycles, and
ordinary homology forgets that the killing happened through singular points.

## The idea: restrict the chains

Intersection homology modifies the chain complex. It does not smooth the space, resolve it, or
throw away the singular points. It only changes which chains are allowed.

Suppose $X$ has dimension $n$ and $S$ is a singular stratum of codimension $k$. A generic
$i$-dimensional chain should meet $S$ in dimension about $i-k$. If $i \lt k$, it should miss $S$
entirely. Intersection homology allows a little extra incidence, governed by a function
$\bar p$ called a perversity:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\dim(\text{chain}\cap S) \leq i-k+\bar p(k).`} />

The larger $\bar p(k)$ is, the more an $i$-chain is allowed to run into codimension-$k$ singular
strata.

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/blog/intersection-homology/figure-3.png" alt="Lower and upper middle perversity rules near an isolated point." />
  <figcaption>The same geometric behavior can be forbidden for one perversity and allowed for another.</figcaption>
</figure>

Despite the dramatic name, a perversity is just a bookkeeping device:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\bar p : \{2,3,4,\ldots\} \to \ZZ, \qquad \bar p(2)=0,`} />

with the rule that $\bar p(k+1)$ is either $\bar p(k)$ or $\bar p(k)+1$. The standard examples are
the zero perversity, the top perversity, and the two middle perversities:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\bar 0(k)=0, \qquad \bar t(k)=k-2,`} />
<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\bar m(k)=\left\lfloor {k-2 \over 2}\right\rfloor,\qquad
\bar n(k)=\left\lceil {k-2 \over 2}\right\rceil.`} />

They satisfy $\bar m(k)+\bar n(k)=k-2$. More generally, perversities $\bar p$ and $\bar q$ are
called complementary if

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\bar p(k)+\bar q(k)=k-2 \quad \text{for every } k\geq 2.`} />

## Allowable chains

Let

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`X=X_n \supset X_{n-2}\supset X_{n-3}\supset \cdots \supset X_0\supset X_{-1}=\varnothing`} />

be a stratification of an $n$-dimensional pseudomanifold. The missing $X_&#123;n-1&#125;$ is intentional:
pseudomanifolds have no codimension-one singular stratum.

If $\sigma:\Delta^i\to X$ is a singular simplex, say that $\sigma$ is $\bar p$-allowable if, for
every codimension-$k$ stratum, equivalently for each $X_&#123;n-k&#125;$ in the filtration,

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\dim\bigl(\sigma^{-1}(X_{n-k})\bigr)\leq i-k+\bar p(k).`} />

Here a negative upper bound means the inverse image must be empty.

A chain is allowable if all its simplices are allowable. The intersection chain group
$IC_i^&#123;\bar p&#125;(X)$ consists of the $i$-chains $\xi$ such that both $\xi$ and its boundary
$\partial \xi$ are $\bar p$-allowable. The boundary condition is what makes this into a chain
complex:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`\cdots \to IC_{i+1}^{\bar p}(X) \xrightarrow{\partial}
IC_i^{\bar p}(X) \xrightarrow{\partial} IC_{i-1}^{\bar p}(X)\to\cdots.`} />

Then

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`IH_i^{\bar p}(X;\QQ) := H_i\bigl(IC_*^{\bar p}(X;\QQ)\bigr).`} />

If $X$ is already a manifold, there are no singular strata, so every chain is allowable and
$IH_i^&#123;\bar p&#125;(X;\QQ)=H_i(X;\QQ)$. Intersection homology is not a replacement for ordinary
homology on manifolds. It is an extension designed to keep manifold-like duality on singular
spaces.

## Poincaré duality, repaired

The central theorem is the following.

<Theorem type="theorem" label="Poincaré duality for intersection homology.">

Let $X$ be a compact, oriented, $n$-dimensional stratified pseudomanifold. If $\bar p$ and
$\bar q$ are complementary perversities, then there is a nondegenerate pairing

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`IH_i^{\bar p}(X;\QQ)\times IH_{n-i}^{\bar q}(X;\QQ)\longrightarrow \QQ.`} />

When $X$ is a manifold, this is ordinary Poincaré duality.

</Theorem>

Why does complementarity appear? Suppose an $i$-chain is allowed to meet a codimension-$k$
stratum in dimension at most $i-k+\bar p(k)$, and an $(n-i)$-chain is allowed to meet it in
dimension at most $(n-i)-k+\bar q(k)$. Inside the stratum, whose dimension is $n-k$, the expected
dimension of their intersection is bounded by

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`(i-k+\bar p(k))+((n-i)-k+\bar q(k))-(n-k).`} />

If $\bar p+\bar q=\bar t$, this becomes at most $-2$. So, after putting chains in stratified
general position, complementary allowable cycles do not create hidden zero-dimensional
intersections inside the singular strata. The intersection number is controlled by what happens in
the top-dimensional smooth part.

The nondegeneracy is deeper. Locally near a point of a codimension-$k$ stratum, the space looks
like $\RR^&#123;n-k&#125;\times cL$, where $L$ is the link. The key calculation is the cone formula:
below a perversity-dependent cutoff, intersection homology of a cone is inherited from the link;
above the cutoff, allowable chains can be pushed into the cone point and become trivial. This is
exactly the mechanism the torus suspension is trying to teach us.

## Back to $\Sigma T^2$

For $X=\Sigma T^2$, the singularities are isolated points in a $3$-dimensional space, so the only
interesting perversity value is $\bar p(3)$.

For the lower-middle perversity, $\bar m(3)=0$. A $2$-chain is allowed to hit an isolated singular
point only if

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`0\leq 2-3+\bar m(3)=-1,`} />

which is impossible. So lower-middle allowable $2$-chains cannot pass through either cone point.
This blocks the ordinary cone filling of a loop, and the two basic loop classes on $T^2$ survive in
$IH_1^&#123;\bar m&#125;$.

For the upper-middle perversity, $\bar n(3)=1$. Now a $2$-chain may meet an isolated singular
point in dimension zero:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`0\leq 2-3+\bar n(3)=0.`} />

The corresponding suspended loops define the complementary $2$-dimensional classes. The cone
calculation gives:

| $i$ | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $IH_i^&#123;\bar m&#125;(X;\QQ)$ | $\QQ$ | $\QQ^2$ | $0$ | $\QQ$ |
| $IH_i^&#123;\bar n&#125;(X;\QQ)$ | $\QQ$ | $0$ | $\QQ^2$ | $\QQ$ |

Now the missing duality is back:

<MathBlock tex={String.raw`IH_1^{\bar m}(X;\QQ)\cong \QQ^2
\quad\text{pairs perfectly with}\quad
IH_2^{\bar n}(X;\QQ)\cong \QQ^2.`} />

Ordinary homology had $H_1=0$ and $H_2=\QQ^2$. Intersection homology restores balance by
remembering which chains used the singularity too aggressively.

## Where the story goes next

This post used chains, but the modern theory is often sheaf-theoretic. The same information is
packaged into the intersection complex $IC_X$, a perverse sheaf whose Verdier self-duality is the
sheaf-theoretic form of Poincaré duality for intersection homology.

That viewpoint leads to the decomposition theorem, the topology of complex algebraic varieties,
representation theory, and the appearance of intersection cohomology in geometric Langlands.
In the next post, we'll go from intersection chains to the intersection complex, and explain why perverse
sheaves are the correct modern framework.
